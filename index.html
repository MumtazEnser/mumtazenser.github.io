Research

My work develops a relational theory of responsibility for complex institutional and socio-technical systems. Drawing on Wittgenstein, Kant, and Arendt, I argue that the so-called "responsibility gap" in AI ethics is not a genuine absence of responsibility but a conceptual and ontological mistake — arising from an individualistic model of agency that fails to account for distributed, practice-based structures of action.

Key Concepts
Authority Illusion
The structural misalignment between where decision authority resides and where accountability is assigned in AI governance architectures.
Ethical Withdrawal
The displacement of responsibility onto systems, procedures, or institutions — allowing actors to continue action while responsibility becomes anonymous.
Relational Agency
Agency understood not as a property of isolated individuals but as emerging within shared practices, institutional roles, and socio-technical structures.
Distributed Responsibility
Responsibility structured across institutional roles, decision architectures, and rule-governed practices rather than concentrated in a single agent.
Systemic Pathology
Harmful outcomes produced not by individual wrongdoing but by the normal operation of systems whose structures generate harm.
Ethical Homeostasis
The regulatory function of ethical norms and governance structures in stabilizing practices and preventing systemic breakdown.
Authority Mapping
A framework for tracing decision authority across strategic, design, governance, and operational layers of AI system deployment.
Epistemic Responsibility
Institutional responsibility for the production, validation, and distribution of knowledge in AI-mediated societies.
