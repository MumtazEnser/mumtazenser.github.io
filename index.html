I work at the intersection of AI ethics, philosophy of technology, Wittgensteinian rule-following, Kantian judgment, social ontology, and institutional responsibility.

Artificial intelligence does not make decisions. What is called “algorithmic decision-making” is often a misdescription of human and institutional practices that authorize, implement, and normalize system outputs.

Theoretical Framework

A unified account of how responsibility, authority, and judgment are displaced in contemporary socio-technical systems.

Algorithms Do Not Decide

Algorithms generate outputs. Decisions occur when outputs are taken up within human and institutional practices that authorize and act upon them.

AI ethicsagency

Authority Illusion

The authority of a system is mislocated at the level of its output, obscuring the upstream choices that make the output actionable.

authoritygovernance

Ethical Withdrawal

Visible harm ceases to function as a reason for action when agents treat procedures, models, or institutional rules as ethically sufficient.

ethicsjudgment

Protean Responsibility

Responsibility is not lost in complex systems; it changes shape across design, deployment, threshold-setting, implementation, and oversight.

responsibilitysystems
